In your case, the local difficulty is that 3D painting is not one action. It is really four things happening at once: the user is choosing a target on the object, choosing a material or channel to affect, choosing a brush behavior, and judging the result through a 3D view. If the interface does not make those four states visible, the user gets lost very quickly.
Your sketches already contain the right basic instinct. The center should be the 3D object, because that is where attention belongs. A left side for action and a right side for state also makes sense. The course material you uploaded pushes exactly in that direction: first identify the user and their primary tasks, then explore with low-fidelity sketches, then refine through wireframes and evaluation rather than polishing too early  . That is the right process here.

The first design decision is to separate actions from context. The left side should answer, “What am I doing right now?” The right side should answer, “What am I painting, and where does it go?” If both sides mix tools, layer stacks, material previews, AI suggestions, and object hierarchy all together, the interface will feel dense and arbitrary.

So I would turn your application into three main zones. The center is the viewport, large and dominant. The left is a narrow tool rail plus a contextual tool-options panel. The right is a semantic panel for object parts, materials, channels, and layers. In practice, that means the user clicks Brush on the left, sees brush radius/flow/hardness/projection settings in the adjacent options column, paints in the center, and checks the active material, texture channels, and layer stack on the right.

That distinction matters because different information changes at different speeds. Tool choice changes often. Brush radius changes constantly. Object hierarchy and material assignments change less often. The interface should reflect that tempo. Fast controls should be close to the cursor and visually light. Structural controls should be stable and always in the same place.

If I translate your sketch into a more concrete version, I would make the leftmost vertical strip extremely simple: Select, Brush, Erase, Fill, Mask, Stencil/Projection, Smudge, Material Picker, UV/Edit Seams, and AI Assist. No text by default, just icons with labels on hover. Then the second strip becomes contextual. For Brush, it shows size, opacity, flow, spacing, falloff, symmetry, pressure mapping, projection mode, and channel toggles. For Fill, it shows flood region, tolerance, target layers, and mask options. For AI Assist, it shows prompt, reference image, target region, generation type, and acceptance controls. This follows the “lots of prompting for low experience, concise commands for high experience” principle from your ideation slides: beginners need visible affordances, while experienced users need efficient access rather than explanation .

The center viewport should be treated as a workspace, not as a passive preview. That means it should carry a few lightweight overlays directly on top of the model: brush cursor, symmetry plane indicator, current target channel, temporary material preview toggle, and maybe a small floating HUD for size/opacity that appears while dragging hotkeys. Do not permanently fill the viewport with panels. Users should feel they are painting the object, not managing a dashboard.

The right side is where your second sketch is strongest. “Parts, Material, Layers” is exactly the correct family of information, but it should be ordered carefully. I would put Parts at the top, Material/Channels in the middle, and Layers at the bottom. The reason is operational. First the user decides what part of the model they are affecting. More concretely, the Parts section should be a tree: Body, Strap, Buckle, Stitching, Eyes, Tail, and so on. It should support solo, lock, hide, and quick selection from the viewport. The Material section should show the active material preview and its channels: base color, roughness, metallic, normal, height, emissive, opacity, whatever your pipeline supports. The Layers section should then be per-channel or shared, depending on your rendering model. The key point is that the user must always be able to answer, at a glance, “I am painting the roughness of the leather material on the strap, on Layer 3.” If that sentence cannot be reconstructed instantly from the UI, the design is not finished.

The prototyping material explicitly distinguishes early focus on high-level concepts from later, more specific layout and look-and-feel decisions , and here the high-level concept is clear: model-first, UV-second.

Now for the harder part: AI. The mistake many interfaces make is to add AI as a separate magic tab. That usually creates mode confusion. The better model is to treat AI as an assistant attached to existing actions. In other words, AI should appear where users already have intent.

There are four AI entry points that make real sense in a 3D painting tool. First, material generation: “Generate a worn yellow lacquer with white scratches” from text or a reference image, producing a starter PBR set. Second, semantic masking: “Select metal buckles,” “isolate seams,” “mask raised areas,” or “segment paws versus cloth.” Third, local paint assistance: texture continuation, fill completion, edge-aware cleanup, seam fixing, and stroke stabilization. Fourth, style or variation suggestions: “make this match the backpack,” “create three roughness variations,” or “age this surface more.” These are useful because they reduce tedious labor while preserving direct control.

The important design rule is that AI outputs should land inside the normal material and layer system. An AI-generated rust mask should become a mask layer the user can inspect, hide, edit, and erase. An AI-generated material should appear as a material asset with editable parameters and textures. If AI writes directly into the object with no trace, users will not trust it. If it becomes just another editable artifact, it becomes usable.

So I would make the AI interface small and local. A compact “Assist” panel can live in the contextual left options strip. It might offer actions like Generate Material, Generate Mask, Inpaint Region, Match Reference, and Describe Selection. Each action asks for a target, such as selected part, selected material, selected UV island, or painted mask. Then the result appears as a non-destructive proposal with Accept, Blend, or Send to New Layer. This keeps AI subordinate to the painting workflow instead of replacing it.

There is also a deeper user-experience issue here. For your application, I would define at least three personas. One is a beginner or hobbyist who wants to recolor and stylize an asset quickly. One is a more technical artist who needs layered control over channels and materials. One is a concept-oriented user who wants fast AI-assisted exploration before manual cleanup. If you do not design for these separately, you will end up with one overloaded interface that satisfies none of them.

From those personas, you get representative tasks. For example: recolor only the bag strap without touching the body; paint dirt only in creases using a mask; generate a starter leather material from a prompt and then manually refine roughness; project a decal onto a curved surface and fix stretching; export the final texture set. Those are the tasks your interface has to make easy. Your course files recommend working with personas, scenarios, and explicit usability criteria rather than vague taste judgments .

This leads naturally to evaluation. You should not jump from sketch to full implementation. Make three low-fidelity variants first. One can be your current left-tools/right-state layout. Another can put materials at the bottom and layers on the right. A third can use workspace tabs such as Paint, Materials, UV, and AI. Then do a quick heuristic review using standard checks like visibility of system status, user control, consistency, recognition over recall, and minimalist design . After that, run a small think-aloud usability test with a few people doing concrete tasks, because the goal is not whether the UI looks nice, but whether they can actually complete representative operations in context .

A good benchmark task set would be something like this. Change the active material’s base color. Paint scratches only on the buckle. Create a new rust mask from AI and edit it manually. Switch to roughness and make the cloth less glossy. Export textures. Then you measure time, visible confusion, wrong-target errors, undo frequency, and subjective clarity.

Right: parts, materials/channels, layers. AI is not a giant new region; it is a contextual assistant mode within the same structure. The top bar should stay thin and contain file actions, undo/redo, workspace switching, and maybe a command/search box. That is enough.

The biggest risk for this application is not lack of features. It is hidden state. Users will constantly ask themselves: which object, which material, which channel, which layer, which mask, which projection mode? Your interface succeeds if those answers are always visible and change in predictable places. Once that is stable, brushes, materials, and AI features can grow without the whole thing collapsing.

Yes. The right side is the part that prevents the painting workflow from becoming ambiguous.
What that panel is supposed to do is not “show extra settings.” Its real job is to keep the user oriented about the target of the edit. In a 3D painting tool, a stroke is never just a stroke. It always means something like: paint this object part, on this material, in this channel, on this layer, possibly through this mask. The right panel should make that chain explicit.

So I would think of the right side as a stack of four related levels, from coarse to fine. At the top, “what object region am I working on?” Then “what material is assigned there?” Then “what texture channel of that material is active?” Then “what editable layer inside that channel am I modifying?” If those four levels are visually separated and always visible, the whole application becomes much easier to understand.

Let me make that concrete.

At the very top of the right panel, I would put a Parts section. This is a semantic tree of the 3D asset. Not triangles, not low-level mesh buffers, but meaningful units: Body, Strap, Buckle, Eyes, Cloth Trim, Stitching, Tail, and so on. The user should be able to click one of these parts and immediately see three things happen. First, the selected part is highlighted in the viewport. Second, the materials section updates to show the materials assigned to that part. Third, the layers section updates to the currently active material and channel for that part.

The reason Parts must come first is that many users do not think in terms of materials initially. They think, “I want to paint the buckle,” not “I want to edit material slot 3.” So the UI should start from the mental model the user already has. This also gives you clean controls like hide, solo, lock, and select-from-viewport. Hide removes the part visually. Solo isolates it. Lock prevents accidental painting. Select-from-viewport means the user can click directly on the model and the Parts tree jumps to the corresponding node. That synchronization is important. The tree and the viewport must never drift apart.

Below that, I would put the Materials section. This is where the right side shifts from object structure to surface definition. Suppose the selected part is “Strap.” The Materials section might show that Strap uses a material called Leather_01. If the selected part is more complex, it may show several material slots. For example, a backpack flap might use a leather material, a metal rivet material, and a stitching material. The user chooses the active material here.

This section should not just be a name in a list. It should look like a compact card. The card shows a thumbnail sphere or patch preview, the material name, and a few summary attributes: base color, roughness behavior, metallic state, maybe whether normal and height maps are present. Think of it as a quick identity card for the surface. If the user changes the active material, that identity card updates, and the viewport reflects the change immediately.

Now comes the most important sub-layer: channels. A physically based material is not one image. It is a collection of related signals. Base color answers “what is the diffuse/albedo-like color?” Roughness answers “how broad or sharp are the highlights?” Metallic answers “does this behave like metal or dielectric?” Normal adds small geometric detail without changing the mesh. Height or displacement may shift surface relief. Emissive controls light emission. Opacity handles transparency or cutout. So inside the Materials section, I would make the channels visible as tabs or a segmented list.

This matters because when people paint 3D surfaces, a huge amount of confusion comes from not knowing which channel is currently active. If I think I am painting color but I am actually painting roughness, the system feels broken. So the active channel should be visually obvious. The UI should say, in effect, “Active target: Strap → Leather_01 → Roughness → Layer 2.” That sentence is the core of the right panel.

Once a channel is active, the next section is Layers. This is the non-destructive editing stack. It should look somewhat familiar to people who know Photoshop or Substance Painter, but adapted to 3D materials. A layer is not just “some paint.” It is an operation or contribution that modifies the current channel. For example, in the roughness channel, one layer might add wear to edges, another might add grime to recesses, another might be hand-painted gloss breakup. In the base color channel, one layer might be the flat leather color, another might be stains, another scratches, another decal projection.

Each layer row should carry the minimum information needed to understand it. That means visibility, lock, name, thumbnail, blend mode, opacity, and maybe an icon for whether it has a mask. If you want the system to scale, layers should also support folders or groups, because complex materials become unreadable otherwise. But I would not start with too much complexity. A good first version is flat layers plus masks.

The crucial design detail is whether layers are shared across channels or channel-specific. There are two possible models.

The first model is channel-specific layers. That means if the user is in Base Color, they see only the base-color layer stack. If they switch to Roughness, they see a different layer stack. This is simpler conceptually and maps cleanly to “I am editing this channel.” The downside is that the relationship between corresponding color and roughness edits becomes less obvious.

The second model is material-wide layers with per-channel outputs. In that design, a single layer can affect multiple channels at once. For example, a “Leather Wear” layer might brighten color slightly, increase roughness variation, and perturb normals all in one semantic operation. This is more powerful and closer to how advanced texturing tools often work, but it is harder for beginners.

For your application, I would start with a hybrid mental model but present it simply. Let each layer be semantic and able to affect multiple channels, but when the user is viewing a given channel, emphasize the contribution of the layers to that channel. In other words, the user can still think “I am on roughness,” while advanced users can create higher-level material effects that touch several channels consistently.

Now let us talk about what happens when the user clicks a layer. Once a layer is selected, a lower subpanel in the right area should become the Properties inspector for that layer. This inspector is where you show the parameters of the currently selected thing. If the selected item is a paint layer, the inspector shows blend mode, opacity, projection mode, whether it clamps values, and mask controls. If the selected item is a procedural dirt layer, the inspector shows noise scale, curvature influence, cavity sensitivity, directionality, and so on. If the selected item is an AI-generated mask, the inspector shows prompt, seed, affected region, and a confidence or refinement control.

This is a very important separation: the stack tells you what exists, and the inspector tells you how the selected thing behaves. If you dump all parameters directly into each layer row, the right side becomes unusable.

So, operationally, the right panel has this internal flow:

First, choose a part.
Then choose the material on that part.
Then choose the channel within that material.
Then choose the layer inside that channel or material stack.
Then adjust the selected layer’s properties in the inspector below.

That ordering is not arbitrary. It follows the logic of narrowing the target. From “which object region?” down to “which editable contribution?” This is what keeps the system legible.

Let me walk through a concrete example, because that is where the mechanism becomes clear.

Suppose the user has a stylized backpack model. They click directly on the metal buckle in the viewport. The Parts section highlights Buckle. In Materials, the active material becomes Metal_Brass. The channels show Base Color, Roughness, Metallic, Normal. The user clicks Roughness. The layer stack now shows maybe Base Finish, Finger Smudges, Edge Polish. The user selects Edge Polish. In the inspector below, they adjust opacity and mask softness. Then they paint directly in the viewport with the brush. The stroke is now unambiguous: it affects the roughness of the brass buckle, specifically in the Edge Polish layer. If the result is wrong, the user knows exactly where to look. That is what a good right panel buys you.

Now compare that with a bad design. If the right panel only says “Current Material: Brass” and shows some sliders, while layers are hidden somewhere else and the active channel is unclear, then the user paints, sees an odd result, and has no idea whether the problem is the selected object, the material slot, the channel, or the layer. That is the failure mode you are trying to eliminate.

There is another subtle issue: preview modes. The right panel should let the user preview not only the full material but also individual channels. This is extremely useful in texture authoring. Sometimes color looks fine, but roughness is wrong. Sometimes normals are too strong. So near the channel selector, I would add preview toggles like “show isolated channel,” “solo selected layer,” and “compare before/after.” These do not need to occupy much space, but they matter for inspection. A material tool without inspection support becomes guesswork very quickly.

You also asked about a more challenging setup with AI. The right side is actually the best place to integrate AI in a controlled way, because it already has the semantic hierarchy of the edit target.

For example, imagine an AI-generated material workflow. The user selects the Strap part, then the Leather_01 material. In the Materials section there is an action like Generate Variant. The user types “aged yellow leather with slight cracking.” The AI proposes a new material variant. Instead of overwriting the existing one invisibly, the right panel shows a new candidate material card or a new grouped layer set under the current material. The user can inspect the generated base color, roughness, and normal contributions separately. This keeps AI outputs inspectable.

Or imagine AI-based masking. The user selects the Body part and clicks Generate Mask in the Layers section. They ask for “dust in creases” or “highlight exposed edges.” The result appears as a mask attached to a new layer. In the inspector, the user sees the prompt, threshold, blur, and inversion controls. Again, AI becomes just another editable object in the existing stack. That is the right design. AI should produce layers, masks, or materials inside the same structure the rest of the app uses.

There is also a strong case for adding an Assets subsection, either as a collapsible area in the right panel or as a switchable tab. This is where saved materials, smart brushes, decals, procedural generators, and AI-generated presets live. But I would keep that separate from the active target stack. Assets are resources you can apply. Parts, materials, channels, and layers describe the current edit context. Mixing those two concepts is a common UI mistake.

So if I compress the right side into a clean hierarchy, it would look like this in terms of meaning, not graphics:

At the top: selection context. Which part is active?
Then: material context. Which material belongs to that part?
Then: channel context. Which surface signal am I editing?
Then: edit context. Which layer or mask am I modifying?
At the bottom: inspector. What are the parameters of that selected layer, mask, or material?

That hierarchy solves most of the conceptual burden.

There are also a few small but high-value details I would add. First, always show a breadcrumb at the top of the right panel, something like Body > Leather_01 > Roughness > Scratches. Second, make locked or hidden states obvious. Third, let users drag materials from an asset library onto parts in the Parts tree or directly onto the viewport. Fourth, provide a tiny real-time preview sphere or patch for the selected material, because a flat thumbnail is often not enough to judge roughness or normal detail. Fifth, allow multi-selection carefully, but do not make it the default. Beginners get lost when one stroke may affect several targets.

If you want a practical first implementation, I would not build the most ambitious version immediately. Start with this simpler right panel:

A Parts tree with hide, solo, and lock.
A Current Material card with a thumbnail preview.
A row of channel tabs: Color, Roughness, Metallic, Normal.
A layer stack for the active channel.
A property inspector for the selected layer.

That is already enough to make the workflow coherent. After that, you can add masks, smart materials, grouped layers, and AI generation.

The bigger picture is that the right panel is the semantic memory of the application. The center is where the user acts, the left is how they act, and the right is what that action means. If you design the right side well, the whole app starts to feel reliable rather than magical. That is the difference between a toy painting demo and a usable authoring tool.

Several is the safer answer.
A “part” and a “material” are two different kinds of things, and it helps to separate them cleanly before deciding how the UI should behave.

A part is a semantic region of the object: strap, buckle, ear, body, sole, zipper, lens frame. It answers “what object region am I talking about?” A material answers “how does that region look and respond to light?” It includes one or more channels such as base color, roughness, metallic, normal, and so on.

Now the local question is: if I select one part in the UI, should that part map to exactly one material, or can it contain several? In a real tool, both situations occur, but if you force only one material per part, you will quickly run into artificial limitations.

Take a small example. Suppose your object is a backpack.

If your parts are defined coarsely as
Body
Strap
Buckle

then maybe each of these can indeed have one material:
Body -> Cloth
Strap -> Leather
Buckle -> Metal

That is the simplest case, and it feels clean.

But now take a slightly richer object. Suppose “Body” includes cloth, stitching, a logo patch, and a plastic zipper border. If you insist that Body has exactly one material, then one of two bad things happens.

Either you split the object into many tiny parts just to satisfy the material system, which clutters the Parts tree and destroys the nice semantic hierarchy.

Or you keep Body as one part, but then you cannot assign different surface definitions inside it, which is too restrictive.

So in practice, a part often needs to support several material assignments.

The clean mental model is this:

A part is a semantic container.
Inside that part, one or more surface regions can each have a material assignment.

So it is often better to think

part -> one or more material slots

rather than

part -> exactly one material

Imagine a stylized shoe.

The user might think in terms of parts like:
Upper
Sole
Laces
Eyelets

Now consider the “Upper.” It may contain:
fabric panels
leather trim
rubber logo
stitched seams

Upper

Fabric_01
Leather_Trim
Rubber_Logo
Stitching_Thread
That is a very reasonable structure.

There is also a technical reason for allowing several materials per part. In 3D assets, material boundaries are often tied to mesh subsets, face sets, material slots, or submeshes. Those boundaries do not always coincide perfectly with the semantic notion of a “part.” A single logical part may be composed of several mesh regions, each with different shading needs. Your UI should not force the semantic structure and the rendering structure to be identical, because they are related but not the same.

That said, there is a tradeoff. If every part can contain arbitrarily many materials, the UI can become confusing. So the trick is not “allow everything visibly all the time.” The trick is to expose complexity only when needed.

I would recommend this behavior.

At the top level, the Parts panel shows semantic parts only. When the user selects a part, the Materials section below shows the material or materials assigned to that part.

If the part has only one material, the UI stays simple. It just shows one material card.

If the part has several materials, the UI expands into a list of material slots for that part.

So the common simple case remains simple, while the richer case is still supported.

In other words, the interface should behave like this:

If selected part has one material:
“Current material: Leather_01”

If selected part has several:
“Materials on this part:
Leather_01
Stitching_Thread
Metal_Ring”

That is much better than forcing a universal one-to-one rule.

There is an even deeper distinction that helps here: ownership versus usage.

A material usually should not be thought of as “belonging” to one part. It is often an asset that can be reused across many parts. For example, the same brass material might be used on several buckles, rings, or decorative studs. So the more accurate relationship is:

a part uses one or more materials,
and a material can be used by one or more parts.

That is many-to-many at the asset level, even if a specific face region has only one active material assignment at a time.

This matters in the UI because if the user edits Brass_Polished, all parts using that material may update. Sometimes that is exactly what they want. Sometimes they want to make a local variation instead. So the UI should support both “edit shared material” and “make unique copy for this part.”

That is a very important operation in material-painting software.

For example:
Buckle_A uses Brass_Polished
Buckle_B uses Brass_Polished

If the user darkens the base color of Brass_Polished, both buckles change.

If the user only wants Buckle_A to differ, they need a command like “duplicate material for this part” or “make local variant.”

So the material system is not only about how many materials a part can have. It is also about whether those materials are shared instances or unique local copies.

For a first version of your app, I would recommend this rule set:

A part may have one or more material assignments.
Each local surface region is assigned exactly one material at a time.
Materials are reusable assets and may be shared across parts.
If a user wants a per-part variation, offer “make unique material” or “create material variant.”

That gives you a system that is both realistic and manageable.

If you want the smallest nontrivial example, here it is.

Suppose Part = “Headband”

Case 1:
Headband -> Leather_Black

Simple, one material.

Case 2:
Headband -> Leather_Black, Metal_Studs

Still one semantic part, but two materials.

Case 3:
Left and right headbands both use Leather_Black

Now editing the material changes both.

Case 4:
User wants only left headband to be worn out more

So they duplicate:

and assign that only to the left part.

That is the workflow your UI should support.

So my recommendation is: do not design the system around “each part has exactly one material.” Design it around “a part usually has one material, but may have several.” Then keep the interface simple by only exposing the extra complexity when it actually exists.

The larger picture is that parts are for human understanding, while materials are for surface definition. Those two structures overlap, but they are not identical. Your UI will be much more robust if you preserve that distinction.

What you are proposing is a strict semantic tree:

the asset is decomposed into parts,

That immediately removes one of the biggest sources of ambiguity in material-authoring tools, namely the question “am I editing one of several materials on this region, or did I select the wrong material slot?” In your model, when I select a part, there is one active material. The main thing to get right is what a “part with subparts” means operationally. There are two possible interpretations.

The first interpretation is that every node in the tree can own geometry directly. In that case, a parent part may have its own faces and material, while also containing child parts with their own faces and materials. That is valid, but it creates one subtle ambiguity: when I click the parent, am I trying to paint the parent’s own surface, or am I conceptually trying to address the whole subtree?

The second interpretation is that internal nodes are mostly semantic groups, and the actual paintable surfaces are the leaves. In that case, a parent part organizes children, but each leaf part is the true material owner.

For your UI, I would recommend a slightly disciplined version of your model:

A part may have children, but every surface triangle belongs to exactly one paintable part. In practice, that means the actual editable target is usually a leaf node. A parent node is still meaningful, but mostly as an organizational and selection unit.

That gives you a very clear invariant. If the user paints, the system knows exactly which PBR material instance is being edited, because the selected paint target is one part, and that part has one material.

A small example makes this clearer. Suppose your stylized cat asset is organized like this:

Cat
├── Body
│   ├── Fur
│   ├── BellyPatch
│   └── Tail
├── Hat
│   ├── Felt
│   └── Band
└── Bag

    └── Buckle

This is a more powerful representation of what artists often mean by a “material layer.” A “Dust” layer might slightly desaturate color, raise roughness, and maybe fill crevices through a mask. A “Polish” layer might darken color a bit, reduce roughness, and sharpen some normal detail. A “Rust” layer might change color, roughness, metallic, and height together. One conceptual operation becomes one layer.

This is closer to how people think about surfaces, but it is less transparent. If the user sees a weird highlight, is it because of a roughness-only layer, or because a higher-level dirt layer is also changing roughness? The system is more expressive, but more hidden.

The third option is a hybrid system, and this is the one I think deserves the most attention for your case.

In the hybrid model, the material still exposes explicit channels like Base Color, Roughness, Metallic, Normal, and so on, but layers can be of two kinds.

One kind is channel-local: “this is a roughness paint layer,” “this is a normal detail layer,” “this is a base color decal.”

The other kind is semantic multi-channel: “this is a leather wear layer,” “this is a dust layer,” “this is a rust generator.”

So the internal data model can represent material-wide contributions, but the UI still lets the user inspect results channel by channel. This is important. It preserves the clarity of the channel view while allowing higher-level authoring.

In practice, the right panel might show a single material layer stack, but each layer row would have badges telling you which channels it affects. For example:

Base Fill — affects Color
Edge Wear — affects Color, Roughness, Normal
Metalness Fix — affects Metallic
Dust — affects Color, Roughness
Scratches — affects Normal, Roughness

Then, when the user clicks the Roughness preview, the stack can either filter visually to emphasize only the roughness contribution or show a channel-solo mode. That gives you inspectability without throwing away semantic grouping.

This hybrid idea is very different from the earlier simpler notion we discussed, where the right side could be “choose channel, then see that channel’s layers.” That earlier idea corresponds to the pure channel-specific model. It is simple and disciplined, and it fits your one-material-per-part structure very well. But it does not capture the fact that many real edits are surface phenomena, not isolated signal edits.

There is another option that sometimes gets conflated with layers, but it is actually different: procedural graphs or node-based material editing. In that model, the final PBR channels are outputs of a graph rather than a stack. The graph may contain noises, blends, masks, triplanar projections, curvature maps, AI-generated textures, and so on.

This is the most general model, because a stack is just a special case of a graph. But for painting-focused interaction, a full graph is usually too much to put at the center of the UI. It is better as an advanced mode or a generated backing representation. If you lead with nodes, you will get enormous flexibility and terrible immediacy. Users who just want to paint a cat ear yellow with rough felt detail will feel like they are authoring a shader compiler.

So I would not make graph editing the primary layer concept, even if procedurally some layers are internally represented that way.

There is also a history-based interpretation of layers. In that design, every stroke or operation is stored as an editable item: brush stroke, fill, projection, AI inpaint, mask generation, procedural dirt pass. That is very appealing from a non-destructive point of view. But it tends to explode in count. Fifty brush strokes become fifty entries. It is powerful for undo and replay, but it is not a good primary authoring abstraction unless you aggressively group strokes into higher-level paint layers.

So I would separate “history” from “layer.” A layer should be a stable editable contribution. A stroke is one operation that may modify that layer.

That distinction matters a lot. If the user says “paint on the scratches layer,” they do not mean “create 800 tiny history nodes.” They mean “the scratches contribution is a named editable surface effect, and my brush adds content into it.”

Now let us compare these options more directly against the earlier idea.

Earlier, once you fixed the model to “one PBR material per part,” the cleanest UI story was:

select part -> see one material -> choose channel -> edit layers for that channel.

This is excellent for legibility. It makes every action explicit. The sentence behind a stroke is something like:

Bag > Leather > Roughness > Scratches Layer

That is extremely good for first implementation and for mental clarity.

The question now is whether that is enough, or whether you want to evolve beyond it.

If your application is mainly about direct texture painting with PBR channels exposed clearly, then channel-specific layers are probably the best first system. They are consistent with your part/material model, easy to explain, easy to render, and easy to debug. They also map naturally onto export, because each channel produces its own texture map or packed texture content.

If, however, you want the app to feel more like a material-authoring environment rather than just a map-painting environment, then pure channel-specific layers will start to feel a bit low-level. Users will want to say “make this part feel dusty” or “add worn edges,” not “open roughness, paint this; now open color, paint that; now open normal, paint again.” That is where semantic multi-channel layers become attractive.

So a useful way to compare the models is by asking what the user thinks they are editing.

In the channel-specific model, the user thinks they are editing signals. Color. Roughness. Normal.

In the material-wide model, the user thinks they are editing surface phenomena. Dust. Wear. Rust. Leather grain.

Those are not the same mental model.

For a technically oriented user, the signal model is often excellent. For a more artistic or exploratory workflow, the phenomenon model is more natural.

There is also a strong AI angle here. AI systems are usually better aligned with semantic layers than with raw channel layers. If the user asks for “aged brass with smudged handling areas,” that is not naturally a base-color-only request or a roughness-only request. It is a material phenomenon request. So if you want to integrate AI in a way that feels coherent, having some notion of semantic material layers is very helpful.

For example, an AI-generated “dust” layer could produce outputs in color and roughness and maybe a mask derived from geometry. That fits naturally in the multi-channel or hybrid model. In a purely channel-specific system, AI would have to either generate separate synchronized layers for each channel or dump results directly into several channel stacks, which is workable but less elegant.

Let me reduce this to a tiny example.

Suppose the selected part is Bag/LeatherFlap.

You want to add edge wear.

In a channel-specific system, you might do this:
you create a Base Color layer named Edge Wear Color,
a Roughness layer named Edge Wear Roughness,
and maybe a Normal layer named Edge Wear Microdetail.

These are separate but conceptually linked. The UI is clear, but the artist has to manage the linkage mentally.

In a material-wide system, you create one layer named Edge Wear, and internally it writes to color, roughness, and normal. The UI matches the semantic idea better, but the user needs inspection tools to understand which channels it affects and by how much.

In a hybrid system, you create one Edge Wear layer, but the UI can still show, when needed, its per-channel contribution. That gives you the best conceptual fit, but also the most design work.

So if I compare “what we had before” to the options now, I would put it like this.

The earlier idea was the clean signal-first design. It is minimal, inspectable, and easy to make correct. It treats layers as belonging to albedo, normal, roughness, and so on. That is still a very strong foundation.

The main limitation of that earlier idea is that it does not naturally group together a single material phenomenon that spans multiple channels. The more advanced alternatives solve that, but at the cost of extra abstraction and UI complexity.

My honest recommendation is not to jump immediately to the most powerful model. I would choose one of these two paths.

The conservative path is to begin with channel-specific layers, but add a concept of linked layer groups. So Edge Wear could be a folder or group that contains a color sublayer, a roughness sublayer, and a normal sublayer. This is very practical. It keeps channels explicit while giving you a semantic grouping mechanism.

The more ambitious path is to build a hybrid material-layer system from the start, where each layer declares which channels it affects, and the UI can flip between “material layer view” and “channel contribution view.” This is the more future-proof design, especially if AI and procedural materials are important to you, but it is also harder to get right.

If I had to formalize a first-version design for your app, I would do this:

Each part has one PBR material.
The material has explicit channels.
The user can inspect channels individually at all times.
The primary editable stack is a material-layer stack.
Each layer may affect one or more channels.
Simple paint layers usually affect one channel.
Higher-level layers, procedural effects, and AI-generated layers may affect multiple channels.
The UI provides a filter or solo view per channel so the user can always inspect the final contribution to albedo, roughness, normal, and so on.

That is basically the hybrid model, but disciplined.

It preserves the clarity of the earlier channel-based idea, because the user can still answer “what is happening in roughness?” But it also lets the application grow into a richer material-authoring system, because not every meaningful edit has to be split manually into separate channel layers.

The central tradeoff is simple. Channel-specific layers maximize inspectability. Material-wide layers maximize semantic coherence. A hybrid system tries to give you both, but only works if the UI is very clear about which channels each layer touches.

So the next real design decision is not “should layers be normal and albedo?” It is: do you want the primary object in the stack to represent a signal edit, or a surface phenomenon? Once you answer that, most of the right-panel design follows naturally.

My own view is that for your application, especially if you want room for AI-assisted materials later, the best long-term answer is a hybrid stack with strong channel inspection. That gives you something richer than the earlier pure-channel idea without sacrificing the ability to debug and reason locally.

For actual authoring tools, I would keep only three to start.

Select is mandatory. It lets the user choose the target part.

Brush is mandatory. It is the core editing action.

Picker is the one extra tool I would strongly recommend. By picker, I mean sampling the current channel value or material appearance from the model. In practice, this prevents the workflow from becoming one-way. Without it, the user paints, but cannot easily inspect or reuse what is already there.

You already mentioned part selection and channel selection. Good. But you are still missing a few things that are not glamorous, yet absolutely central.

You need viewport navigation. Orbit, pan, and zoom are not optional in a 3D painting app. They are not “advanced features”; they are part of using the brush at all.

You need undo and redo. A painting app without immediate recovery is not really usable, especially in a prototype where mistakes are common.

You need visible current target state. The interface should always show something like “Selected part: Bag > Strap” and “Active channel: Roughness.” Otherwise the user will lose track of what they are editing.

You need minimal brush parameters. At least size and strength. Hardness is very useful too, but if you want the absolute minimum, size and strength come first.

You need inspection or preview of the active channel. This one is easy to miss. If the user is painting roughness or normal, the full shaded view alone is often not enough to understand what changed. Even a tiny toggle that says “preview base color / roughness / metallic / normal / shaded” would help a lot.

And you need the part tree to support at least hide or isolate. Lock is nice, but if you have to choose, isolate is often more immediately useful because it helps the user focus on one region.

So if I rewrite the minimal scope more rigorously, I would say the minimal app should support one complete benchmark task: the user can navigate the model, select a part, choose a PBR channel, adjust the brush, paint on that channel, inspect the result, and undo if needed. That is the real minimal unit of usability. The course material on evaluation also stresses using clear benchmark tasks stated in the user’s language, focusing on what the user should achieve rather than how they do it .

In practical UI terms, the minimal first screen I would aim for is this. A top bar with file and undo/redo. A left rail with Select, Brush, Picker, maybe Fill. You do not need to expose every possible PBR channel at first, but those four are the most defensible minimum if you want to claim PBR support. There is one more thing I think you are missing conceptually. You need to distinguish tools from targets from properties.

A tool is Select or Brush.

A target is the selected part and the selected channel.

A property is brush size, strength, and maybe current value or color.

A lot of student prototypes mix these together in the same panel. That makes the interface feel messy even when the feature count is small. Your design will become much stronger if you keep these three categories separate. Left side for tools and tool properties. Right side for targets and material/channel state. Center for action and feedback.

A minimal 3D painting app is already enough of a project.
